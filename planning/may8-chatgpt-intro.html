What it is — and what it isn't

Not a search engine

  • Doesn't look up facts in real-time (mostly)
  • Can hallucinate — confidently wrong
  • Not authoritative on recent events

A very capable thinking partner

  • Synthesises and explains anything you give it
  • Drafts, rewrites, critiques your writing
  • Argues both sides, plays devil's advocate
  • Generates code, figures, structures on demand

The best mental model: a brilliant but forgetful colleague who knows a lot, can reason fast, but you should always double-check their references.

The anatomy of a good prompt

Three elements make the difference between a generic answer and a useful one.

Summarise the key findings from these three abstracts← TASK: what you want done and identify one unresolved question they all share. Context: I am a researcher in ultrafast X-ray spectroscopy preparing a grant proposal for VR. The audience is a review panel with broad physics background.← CONTEXT: who you are, who will read it Format your answer as: - Three bullet points (one finding per paper) - One paragraph on the shared gap - Max 200 words total← FORMAT: shape of the output

You rarely write the perfect prompt on the first try. The skill is iteration — ask, read, refine.

Prompt quality — before and after

Weak prompts

"Summarise this paper"
"Write a grant introduction"
"Explain machine learning"
"Is my analysis correct?"

Strong prompts

"Summarise this paper for a PhD student in optics. Focus on experimental methods. 150 words."
"Write a 200-word VR grant intro for a study on ultrafast dynamics. Emphasise societal impact."
"Explain supervised learning using an analogy from X-ray diffraction."
"Here is my fitting approach [paste]. What assumptions could break it? List 3."

Five things to try this week

1. Literature synthesis

Paste 3–5 abstracts → ask for themes, gaps, and one open question. Saves 30 min of reading.

2. Writing feedback

Paste your draft → ask "what's unclear, what's missing, what should I cut?" Brutal but fair.

3. Grant impact paragraph

Describe your research in 3 sentences → ask it to write a 200-word impact statement for a funding panel.

4. Peer review draft

Paste a paper → ask "what are the 3 strongest weaknesses in the methodology?" Good first pass.

5. Explain your research

Ask it: "I work on [topic]. Explain what I do to a smart 16-year-old, then to a science journalist, then to a VR grant panel." Three very different answers — all useful.

What to watch out for

Hallucination

It will invent plausible-sounding citations, statistics, and facts. Always verify specific claims.

Sycophancy

It tends to agree with you. Push back: "What's wrong with this?" or "Argue against my approach."

Generic output

Vague prompts produce vague text. The more context you give, the more specific and useful the result.

Confidentiality

Don't paste unpublished data, sensitive student info, or confidential grant content unless you've checked KTH's data policy.

Your domain expertise is the quality filter. AI is fast and fluent — you provide the scientific judgment.

Exercise: Literature Synthesis

Open chat.openai.com (or claude.ai) and paste this prompt — then replace the placeholders with your own abstracts:

Here are 3 abstracts from my research field: [ABSTRACT 1 — paste here, or use one from the handout] [ABSTRACT 2 — paste here] [ABSTRACT 3 — paste here] Please: 1. Identify the main shared theme across these papers (2 sentences) 2. Point out one contradiction or unresolved tension between them 3. Suggest one research question none of them has answered yet 4. Draft a 100-word introduction paragraph for a study that addresses that gap Keep the language appropriate for a specialist reader in my field.

Then iterate: ask it to change the tone, make it shorter, push back on its gap suggestion, or ask "what are you most uncertain about in this answer?"

No abstracts handy? Use the three provided on the handout / tutorial page. Work in groups of 2–4.
