02 · what changed

What I actually see when I work with AI now.

The interesting part is not the chatbot. The interesting part is that I now run systems of agents that read code, write code, call tools, send email — and wait for me to disagree.

Context, not prompts

Behaviour is set by a project brief I wrote — conventions, vocabulary, what counts as “done”. The prompt is one line. The brief is the whole game.

Loops, not queries

The first answer is a draft. Real work happens inside an iteration with explicit stop conditions, where I (or another agent) checks what came back.

Aligned vocabulary

What does “fit” mean — least squares, MLE, Bayesian? “Noise” — additive, multiplicative, Poisson? If we drift, the agent fabricates confidently.

A concrete example. Pump-probe kinetics extraction. The prompt is one sentence. The brief that says “biexponential decay, additive Gaussian noise, refuse to fit below SNR 3, report with residuals and confidence intervals” is what separates a useful agent from a confident fabricator. — This is system design, not prompt engineering.

Literature work

End-to-end: download n papers, extract methods, build a comparison table, draft a related-work section with citations. Minutes, not weeks.

The work an agent can do today is roughly the work we currently ask students to do on a problem set, a lab report, or a literature review. That is the entire problem.

04 · what I worry about

Three claims I am willing to defend.

  • A problem set an agent solves in five minutes is no longer measuring whether the student learned. It measures whether they have the agent.
  • A lab report an agent writes from raw data is no longer assessing scientific writing. It is assessing copy-paste.
  • An oral defence with follow-up questioning still works. A live in-room derivation still works. An experiment that requires hands still works.
The good news. The fix is not banning AI. It is changing the format of what we ask — so the thing being assessed is something an agent cannot deliver alone, and so honest use of AI is rewarded rather than hidden.
Pause after the third claim. Let the asymmetry land — written formats fall; live formats hold.

05 · the durable skills

What our graduates must do — that an agent cannot.

  • Frame the problem. Decide what is worth measuring, modelling, arguing for. AI answers. Only humans choose the question.
  • Recognise physically wrong answers. Catch the confident error that is mathematically consistent but physically impossible.
  • Defend the work live, under questioning. If you cannot explain why, you do not own the result.
  • Use AI deliberately and disclose it. Honest use is a skill. Hidden use is a failure mode — for the student and for us.
  • Hold physical intuition. The silent prior the agent does not have, and the only thing that catches the “walk 50 m to a car wash” answer.
Cheap, this year: looking up, first drafts, routine derivations, summarising, reformatting. Scarce, this year: framing, judgment, taste, agency, real-time communication.

06 · designs worth trying

Five exam designs I want to argue for.

Oral defence after written submission. A 15-min follow-up viva. Explain three choices in your report. Rederive one piece live. defends — understanding, ownership.
AI-required, with disclosure. “Use three different agents on this. Argue which is physically meaningful and which is wrong. Cite each prompt.” defends — judgment, honest use.
Lab presentation, not lab report. A 10-min talk plus live Q&A. The text version is a one-page abstract, not a twelve-page document. defends — communication, integration.
Process portfolio. Submit the messy notebook, the AI conversations, the failed fits, the dead ends — not just the polished final output. defends — the learning trajectory.
In-room re-derivation. From your own report, pick a result. Re-derive a slice on the board, now, no laptop. defends — that the work is yours.
06 · designs worth trying
07 · what I am arguing for

Where I stand.

We do not need to ban AI from our courses. We need to redesign the format of what we ask, so the thing being assessed is something an agent cannot deliver alone — and so honest use of AI by the student becomes a rewarded skill, not a hidden one.

Communication, judgment, and physical intuition become the assessment. The product of the agent becomes the raw material.

A practical proposal for autumn 2026. Pick one course per division. Redesign one assessment along these lines. Bring the result to the next teachers' meeting. Real evidence beats more opinion — and we are in a good position to generate it.

I am not pretending I have this right. I want the room to push back.
