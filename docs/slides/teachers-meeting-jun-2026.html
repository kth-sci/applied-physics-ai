Working with agents is not talking to ChatGPT

From the SCI Agenthon Track C reading — three things that actually matter:

Context engineering

Deliberately shaping what the model sees: project notes, file mounts, skills triggered on demand. Garbage in, garbage out — but worse, because the output looks confident.

Loop engineering

Agents don't decide when they are done — you do, with explicit stop conditions. "Everything is a Ralph loop." Night-shift automation by day-shift humans.

Aligned understanding

A shared glossary, explicit specs, named conventions. The AI uses your domain terms or justifies deviating. System-design intuition stays human.

The question is not: what prompt should I write?
The question is: what should Claude know? — SCI Agenthon Track C, "Advanced agentic engineering"

What this looks like in practice

Pick three concrete, citable cases — the rest are hype:

AlphaEvolve breaks a 56-year record

Google DeepMind's Gemini-powered evolutionary coding agent autonomously discovered a procedure to multiply two 4×4 complex matrices using 48 scalar multiplications — the first improvement on Strassen's algorithm since 1969. It also produced new results across analysis, combinatorics, and geometry.

DeepMind blog, May 2025.

A 47-year-old computer running a transformer

Dave Plummer — veteran Windows developer (Task Manager) — wrote a working mini-transformer in PDP-11 assembly running on a 6 MHz, 64 KB-RAM PDP-11 from 1978. A teaching demo of what these models really are.

Tom's Hardware, 2025.

What does the teaching literature say?

Two facts to hold at once:

The aggregate effect is positive

  • Wang et al. (Nature HSSC, 2026) — meta-analysis of 35 experimental studies. g = 0.67 on learning outcomes.
  • Liu et al. (J. Computer Assisted Learning, 2025) — large positive performance effect.
  • HEPI Student GenAI Survey 2026 — majority of UK undergraduates already use AI weekly.

But the nuance is brutal

  • "Outsourcing tasks to GenAI without pedagogical guidance enhances performance with no real learning gains." (Systematic-review framing, 2026)
  • Effects depend sharply on knowledge type and assignment design.
  • One major Nature HSSC meta-analysis was retracted in 2025. The field is moving fast and messily.
Students get higher scores. Whether they learn more — or learn less but cover for it — depends almost entirely on how we design the work.

What I am seriously worried about — the squeeze

The signal in the labour market

  • Entry-level hiring at the 15 largest US tech firms down 25% from 2023 to 2024.
  • Junior developer postings down ~40-50% since early 2024.
  • Employment of devs aged 22-25 down ~20% from late-2022 peak.
  • LeadDev 2025 survey: 54% of engineering leaders plan to hire fewer juniors because of AI copilots.

Stack Overflow Blog (Dec 2025); SSRN, Adam (2026).

The pedagogical implication

The classical career arc was: learn basics → junior role → build expertise. The middle step is what's getting absorbed by agents.

If our graduates can do only what a competent agent can already do, they have no entry point. They have to be measurably better.

Counter-data point: IBM announced tripling US entry-level hiring in 2026. The picture is not uniform. But the trend among software-heavy firms is unmistakable.

What we owe students — and the inequality that matters

Student alone < AI alone < Student + AI

Our graduates can survive only on the right-hand side — and only if "+AI" is more than copy-paste.

Skills that get cheap

  • Looking things up.
  • Writing the first draft of code, text, or a derivation.
  • Routine application of a known method.
  • Boilerplate. Reformatting. Summarising.

Knowledge access has collapsed in price. Expertise is less defendable.

Skills that get scarce — and valuable

  • Problem framing — deciding what is worth asking.
  • Judgment & taste — recognising when the agent is confidently wrong.
  • Physical intuition — the silent prior our students still need.
  • Agency — running a loop end-to-end, not waiting for instructions.
  • Communicating to humans — still done in human time.
You can outsource your thinking, but you cannot outsource your understanding. — quoted by Karpathy, late 2025

Let's actually talk — five questions to debate

  • What is the one course in your programme where you can already see the effect of AI on student work? What changed?
  • If a student turns in work an AI agent could have produced in 5 minutes — have they learned anything we cared about? If not, what should the assignment have asked instead?
  • What is the minimum AI fluency we owe an APHYS graduate by the time they leave us? Should it be a course, an embedded thread, or an examination requirement?
  • Where do we draw the line on disclosure: in homework, exams, theses, grant texts, recommendation letters?
  • What is the experiment our department could run in autumn 2026 that would give us actual evidence — not opinion — about what works?
Today is not the day we decide policy. It is the day we make a list of the experiments worth running — and the questions we now know we have to answer.
