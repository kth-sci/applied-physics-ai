Not a search engine — a thinking partner

What it is NOT

  • A search engine with sources
  • An oracle of facts
  • A one-shot answer machine
  • A replacement for your expertise

What it IS

  • A fast, tireless thinking partner
  • A first-draft generator you refine
  • A sounding board that never judges
  • An amplifier of your domain knowledge

Think: a brilliant but forgetful colleague who has read everything, reasons fast, but sometimes invents plausible-sounding nonsense. Your job is to catch that — and your physics training makes you good at it.

Five patterns that make the difference

① Task Say exactly what you want done
② Context Tell it who you are and why
③ Format Specify the output shape
④ Role Assign it a persona or stance
⑤ Iterate The real skill is the follow-up

You rarely need all five at once. Start with ① and ③, add ② when the output is too generic, use ④ for critical review, and ⑤ always — the first response is rarely the best.

A prompt with all five patterns

Act as a critical peer reviewer for a VR grant application. ← role Read the research description below and identify the three weakest points in the scientific argument. ← task I am a researcher in ultrafast X-ray spectroscopy at KTH Applied Physics. The reviewers will be physicists but not specialists in my sub-field. This is for a 4-year senior researcher grant. ← context Format: numbered list, one sentence per weakness, then a suggested fix for each. Max 200 words. ← format [paste your research description here]

After it replies → iterate: "Make the tone more constructive." / "Which of the three is most critical?" / "Now write the improved version."

Weak vs. strong — physics examples

❌ Too vague

"Summarise this paper"
"Write a grant intro"
"Explain machine learning"
"Is my analysis correct?"
"Help me with my lecture"

✓ Specific and useful

"Summarise for a PhD student in optics — focus on experimental method, 150 words."
"200-word VR grant intro for ultrafast dynamics, non-specialist panel, stress societal impact."
"Explain supervised learning using an analogy from X-ray diffraction."
"Here is my fitting approach [paste code]. List 3 assumptions that could break it."
"I teach quantum mechanics to 3rd-year engineers. Suggest one active-learning exercise on spin that takes 10 min."

When it goes wrong — and it will

Hallucination

Invents citations, statistics, equations. Sounds completely confident. Always verify specific claims — especially paper titles and DOIs.

Sycophancy

Will agree with whatever framing you give it. Counter with: "What is wrong with this argument?" or "Steel-man the opposite view."

Generic drift

Without context, output is textbook-level generic. The more specific your prompt, the more specific — and useful — the response.

Confidentiality

Don't paste unpublished data, student personal info, or grant content you haven't cleared. Check KTH's AI data policy.

Your domain expertise is the quality filter. ChatGPT is fast and fluent — you provide the scientific judgment. This is not optional.

Five things you can do before Monday

Literature synthesis

Paste 3 abstracts → ask for themes, gaps, and one open question. Saves 30 min of reading.

Grant impact paragraph

3-sentence research description → 200-word impact statement for a funding panel.

Writing feedback

Paste your draft → "What's unclear, what's missing, what should I cut?" Brutal but fair.

Peer review draft

Paste a paper → "What are the 3 strongest weaknesses in the methodology?" Good first pass.

Explain your research at every level

"I work on [topic]. Explain it to: a smart 16-year-old, a science journalist, a VR grant panel." Three very different answers — all useful.
