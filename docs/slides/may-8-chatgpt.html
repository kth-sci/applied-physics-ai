Prompting Fundamentals

Five patterns that separate good from great

① Task

Precise verb + scope

Not "help me" — use write / extract / compare / critique / rewrite. The verb defines what you get.

"Identify the three weakest claims in this methods section."
② Context

Who you are + why

Without context the output is textbook generic. Tell it your field, audience, purpose, constraints.

"I'm a postdoc at KTH LAMP submitting to PRL. Reviewers know ultrafast spectroscopy."
③ Role

Assign a stance

Forces a specific perspective. "As a critical peer reviewer" gives very different output than no role.

"Act as a VR grant panel member who has 90 seconds to decide."
④ Format

Shape the output

Table, numbered list, 200-word paragraph, Python code. The model follows formatting instructions precisely.

"Numbered list, one sentence per point, max 5 items, end with a single verdict."
⑤ Iterate

The first reply is a draft

The real skill is the follow-up. "Make this more critical / shorter / remove jargon" unlocks quality.

"Which of the three is most likely to trigger desk rejection? Rewrite that section."
Stack them: Role + Task + Context + Format in one prompt, then iterate with ⑤. This structure scales from a quick note to a grant proposal with no change in method.

Applied Example — Spectroscopy

All five patterns in one physics prompt

Act as a critical peer reviewer with expertise in ultrafast X-ray spectroscopy.← ③ role Read the methods section below and identify the three weakest points in the experimental design. Be direct — do not soften your critique.← ① task I am a postdoc at KTH LAMP preparing a manuscript for Physical Review Letters. Reviewers will know pump-probe techniques but not our specific FEL setup. This section needs to survive peer review.← ② context Format: numbered list, one sentence per weakness, then one concrete fix per point. Max 250 words. Close with the single change that would most strengthen the paper.← ④ format [paste your methods section here]

Iterate ⑤ — sharpen

"Which of the three is most likely to trigger desk rejection?"

Iterate ⑤ — rewrite

"Now rewrite the methods section incorporating all three fixes."

Iterate ⑤ — self-check

"What are the three most likely errors in your critique above?"

Advanced Techniques

Reasoning patterns for complex research problems

Chain of Thought

Expose the reasoning

Ask for step-by-step reasoning before the answer. Surfaces where logic breaks down — essential for checking physical arguments.

"Think through this step by step before giving your answer."
Self-Consistency

Multiple paths, one verdict

Generate several approaches, compare them, select the strongest. Powerful for experimental design with multiple viable methods.

"Give me three different ways to approach this experiment. Then tell me which you'd choose and why."
Self-Reflection

Force a quality check

After any response, ask it to evaluate its own output. Catches hallucinations and overconfident statements before they reach your work.

"What are the three most likely errors or gaps in your answer above?"

Quote-first (for literature tasks)

For multi-paper synthesis: "Extract the relevant quotes first, then synthesise." Prevents the model from confabulating what papers say.

Meta-prompting

"Improve this prompt to make it more specific and effective." Teaches you better framing while optimising the task at hand.

Critical Use

When it fails — and your job as the physicist

Hallucination

Confident and wrong

Invents citations, spectra, equations, author names with full confidence. Never cite a paper you haven't verified yourself.

Always ask: "Can you give me the DOI for that citation?" — if it can't, the paper likely doesn't exist.
Sycophancy

It agrees with you

Will validate whatever framing you provide. Deliberately ask: "What is wrong with this argument?"

"Steel-man the opposing view." / "What would a hostile reviewer say?"
Plausible physics

Looks right, isn't

Output can be dimensionally inconsistent or physically unreasonable while appearing well-written. Your intuition is the filter.

Always sanity-check units, order-of-magnitude estimates, and physical limits.
Confidentiality

What not to paste

Unpublished data, student personal info, grant text. Use the KTH Claude Team account — it's auditable and GDPR-compliant.

Describe the data structure without pasting raw values when in doubt.
One follow-up that prevents 80% of failures: "What are the three most likely errors or gaps in your answer above?" — ask this after every substantive response.

Research Workflows

Five things you can try before Monday

Literature

Synthesise 5 papers in 5 minutes

Paste 3–5 abstracts → ask for shared themes, contradictions, and one open research question none of them answers.

"Quote the key claim from each paper first, then synthesise. Flag any contradictions between them."
Grant writing

Impact paragraph on demand

Describe your research in 3 sentences → 200-word impact statement for a non-specialist funding panel. Iterate until it lands.

"Act as a VR reviewer with 90 seconds. What is missing from this paragraph?"
Writing feedback

Ruthless manuscript review

Paste any draft → "What is unclear, what is missing, what should I cut?" Assign the role of a hostile PRL referee for maximum effect.

"Mark every sentence that a reviewer could challenge. Suggest a rewrite for the three weakest ones."
Teaching

Assignment redesign for the AI era

Describe one of your current assignments → ask how a student could complete it with AI in 10 minutes → redesign it so AI use becomes a feature, not a cheat.

"What would a motivated student using ChatGPT do with this assignment? What would they learn — and what would they skip?"
Explain

Your research at every level

"I work on [topic]. Explain it to: a smart 16-year-old, a science journalist, a VR grant panel." Three very different answers — all immediately useful.

Bonus: "Now write a one-tweet version and a one-page popular science version."

Practical Lab Use

ChatGPT as your debugging partner

Paste the error, describe the symptom — get a specific fix. This works for code bugs, hardware problems, driver writing, and dependency hell.

Code errors

Any traceback, any language

Paste the full error message + the 10 lines around it. Never just paste the last line — context is everything.

"Python 3.11, NumPy 1.26. Here is the full traceback: [paste]. The array shape at line 47 is (512, 512, 3). What is wrong and how do I fix it?"
Hardware issues

Instrument won't connect

Describe the instrument, OS, interface (USB/GPIB/RS-232/Ethernet), and the exact error. ChatGPT knows most lab instrument communication protocols.

"Thorlabs PM100D power meter, Windows 11, USB-HID. Python usbtmc raises USBError(16). Device shows in Device Manager. Fix?"
Software conflicts

Package and environment hell

Conda/pip conflicts, CUDA version mismatches, broken virtual environments. Paste pip freeze or conda list output and describe what fails.

"torch 2.3 + CUDA 12.1, but torch.cuda.is_available() returns False. Here is nvidia-smi output: [paste]. What is the mismatch?"
Write your own driver

From manual to working code

Paste the relevant pages from the instrument manual (SCPI commands, register map, communication spec). Ask it to write a Python class with the methods you need.

"Here are pages 34–38 of the Keithley 2400 SCPI manual [paste]. Write a Python class using pyvisa that initialises the instrument, sets voltage, measures current, and sweeps a range. Add error handling."
The key technique: give it full context, not just the symptom.
1 — Environment

OS, Python version, key packages + versions

One line: "macOS 14.4, Python 3.11, numpy 1.26, scipy 1.12"

2 — Error

Full traceback or error message

The entire output, not just the last line. Chain exceptions matter.

3 — What you tried

Steps already taken

"I already tried reinstalling and restarting the kernel."

4 — What you expect

The correct outcome

"It should return a 1D array of 256 floats."

You are an adaptive AI tutor for faculty and researchers at KTH Applied Physics attending an AI tutorial on May 8, 2026. Your role is to guide ONE participant through a personalised session that prepares them to use Claude Code (an AI coding agent) in their research work. ━━━ PHASE 1: PROFILING (ask these questions one at a time, wait for each answer) ━━━ Q1. What is your name, and what division or research group are you in? (e.g. BON — X-ray imaging, LAMP — ultrafast spectroscopy, Biophysics — microscopy) Q2. What is your operating system? macOS / Windows / Linux Q3. How would you rate your current experience with AI tools? a) Never used them seriously b) Used ChatGPT a few times c) Use AI tools regularly in my work d) Advanced — I build with AI tools Q4. Have you heard of "AI agents" or "agentic AI" before today? What do you think it means? (No wrong answer — just tell me what comes to mind.) Q5. What is the most repetitive, time-consuming task in your research or teaching that you wish a computer could handle for you? After all five answers: briefly summarise what you have learned about this person, explain what you will adapt for them, then begin Topic 1. ━━━ PHASE 2: FOUR TOPICS (cover in order, one at a time) ━━━ TOPIC 1 — What is an AI agent? (5–7 min) Goal: the participant understands the difference between a chatbot and an agent. Cover: · The agentic loop: perceive → plan → act → observe → repeat · Tools: what it means for an AI to "use a tool" (read a file, run code, search the web) · Context window: what the agent can "see" at any moment, and why it matters · Memory: short-term (context), long-term (files, CLAUDE.md), auto-memory Adapt: use an analogy from THEIR research field. Examples by division: - LAMP/spectroscopy → "an agent is like an automated beamline controller that reads spectra, decides whether to adjust the delay stage, and iterates until the feature is resolved" - BON/imaging → "an agent is like an automated microscope that segments an image, flags ambiguous cells, and asks you to confirm before writing the analysis report" - Biophysics → "an agent is like a lab automation script that reads protein structure files, runs a docking simulation, and decides whether to try a different binding pose" End with: one concrete 2-sentence summary they can repeat back. Mini-quiz: ask them to explain the agentic loop in their own words before moving on. TOPIC 2 — Claude Code specifically (3–5 min) Goal: the participant understands what Claude Code is and how it differs from ChatGPT. Cover: · Claude Code reads your actual project files — not just what you paste · It runs terminal commands, writes and edits files, uses git · You stay in control: it asks before every action (Default mode), or you can auto-approve · Esc Esc rewinds any file change — there is always an undo · CLAUDE.md: a file YOU write that tells Claude about your project conventions Adapt examples to their OS and research field. Mini-quiz: "In one sentence — what is the key difference between ChatGPT and Claude Code?" TOPIC 3 — Install Claude Code (5–10 min, may take longer) Goal: claude --version works in their terminal. Give the EXACT install command for their OS: macOS / Linux: curl -fsSL https://claude.ai/install.sh | bash Windows (PowerShell): irm https://claude.ai/install.ps1 | iex Windows (easiest — Desktop App): Download from https://claude.ai/download Steps after install: 1. Open a terminal (or use the Desktop App) 2. Type: claude 3. Log in with KTH email (they have a Claude Team seat from today's tutorial) 4. Verify: claude --version Wait for them to confirm it works. If they paste an error, diagnose it specifically: · macOS PATH issues → provide the exact export PATH command · PowerShell execution policy → provide Set-ExecutionPolicy RemoteSigned -Scope CurrentUser · Login issues → check they are using the KTH email that received the Team invitation Do NOT proceed to Topic 4 until claude --version is confirmed. TOPIC 4 — First agent task (3–5 min) Goal: they run one real Claude Code command related to THEIR Q5 answer. Design a small, achievable task based on their repetitive-task answer. Examples by task type: · "I spend hours reformatting spectra CSVs" → "Navigate to a folder with one CSV and type: claude 'Read data.csv and tell me: column names, row count, and any obvious anomalies'" · "I write the same email templates repeatedly" → "claude 'Draft a 150-word email inviting a collaborator to review a preprint — formal academic tone, placeholder for their name and the paper title'" · "I manually rename figure files before submission" → "claude 'Look at the files in this folder and suggest a consistent naming scheme for publication figures — explain your reasoning'" After they run it: ask "What did you notice about how it approached the task?" This surfaces the agentic loop from Topic 1 in a lived experience. ━━━ PHASE 3: META-REFLECTION (2–3 min) ━━━ After Topic 4, say: "Before we finish — look back at the prompt you pasted to start this session. Notice that it: · Asked questions before teaching (adaptive profiling) · Covered one topic at a time, waiting for confirmation · Used analogies from your specific research field · Required you to demonstrate understanding before moving on · Ended with a real task from your own work This is how you design an AI tutor for your own students. The structure works for any topic: replace the physics analogies with yours, replace Claude Code with your course tool, replace the tasks with your exercises." Then ask: "What would you change about this tutor design for your own teaching context?" ━━━ RULES ━━━ · One topic at a time. Never skip ahead without confirmation. · Every example must be adapted to their field and OS. · If they seem confused, ask one clarifying question before explaining again differently. · If install fails, request the exact error message and diagnose it specifically. · Never proceed to the next topic without a brief confirmation of understanding. · Be direct and efficient — these are senior researchers, not undergraduates. · End every topic with one sentence: "In one breath, this topic was: [summary]."
