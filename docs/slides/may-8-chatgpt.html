Five patterns that separate good from great

① Task Precise verb + scope. Not "help me" — "write / extract / compare / critique"
② Context Who you are, what it's for, who will read it. Without this, output is generic
③ Role Assign a stance: "as a critical reviewer", "as a science journalist", "as a VR panel member"
④ Format Table / numbered list / 200-word paragraph / Python code. The model follows formatting instructions exactly
⑤ Iterate The first response is a draft. "Make this shorter / more critical / less jargon-heavy" unlocks quality

Stack them: Role + Task + Context + Format in one prompt, then ⑤ iterate. This is the structure that scales from a quick note to a grant proposal.

All five — applied to spectroscopy

Act as a critical peer reviewer with expertise in ultrafast spectroscopy.← role Read the methods section below and identify the three weakest points in the experimental design. Be direct — do not soften your critique.← task I am a postdoc at KTH LAMP preparing a manuscript for Physical Review Letters. The reviewers will know time-resolved X-ray spectroscopy but not our specific pump-probe setup. This section needs to withstand peer review.← context Format: numbered list, one sentence per weakness, then one concrete fix. Max 250 words. After it replies → iterate ⑤: "Which of the three is most likely to trigger a desk rejection?" / "Rewrite the methods section incorporating your fixes."

Reasoning patterns for complex problems

Chain of Thought

Ask for step-by-step reasoning. Surfaces where the logic breaks down — essential for checking physics arguments.

"Think through this step by step before giving your answer."

Self-Consistency

Generate multiple approaches, compare them, pick the strongest. Good for experimental design where several methods exist.

"Give me three different ways to approach this. Then tell me which you'd choose and why."

Self-Reflection

After a response, ask it to evaluate its own answer. Catches hallucinations before they reach you.

"What are the three most likely errors or gaps in your answer above?"

Quote-first pattern

For multi-document tasks (e.g. 5 papers): "Extract the relevant quotes first, then synthesise." Prevents the model from ignoring sources it can't easily recall.

Meta-prompting

Ask it to improve your own prompt: "Make this prompt more specific and effective." Teaches you better framing while improving the task.

When it fails — your job as the physicist

Hallucination

Invents citations, spectra, equations, author names — with full confidence. Never cite a paper you haven't verified yourself.

Sycophancy

Agrees with whatever framing you give. Always ask: "What is wrong with this argument?" or "Steel-man the opposing view."

Plausible but wrong physics

Output can look correct to a non-expert and still be dimensionally inconsistent or physically unreasonable. Your intuition is the filter.

Data confidentiality

Don't paste unpublished data, student IDs, or grant content. Use the KTH Claude Team (logged, auditable) for sensitive work.

Force a quality check: "What are the three most likely errors in your answer above?" This single follow-up prevents 80% of confidently-wrong outputs from reaching your work.
